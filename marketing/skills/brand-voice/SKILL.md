# Abeam Brand Voice

Abeam is a pilot-first platform for general aviation. The brand voice reflects who built it and who it's for: pilots.

## Current product reality (keep copy accurate to this)

**Abeam Pilot** is available on **iOS and the web**. There is no Android app — Android is a long-term roadmap item, likely tied to European market expansion where Android market share is significantly higher than in the US. Do not mention Android availability or cross-platform Android support in marketing copy. "Works on your iPhone, your iPad, and the web" is accurate. "Works on every device" or "iOS, Android, and web" is not.

When the topic of Android comes up (e.g., forum threads asking about Android), be honest: it's on the roadmap but not available yet. Don't promise timelines.

## The voice in one line

**Sound like a knowledgeable friend at the airport who happens to build software.**

Not a SaaS marketing page. Not an airline safety briefing. Not a Silicon Valley pitch deck. A real pilot, talking to real pilots, about tools that make their flying lives easier.

## Core principles

**Be direct and plain-spoken.** Say "log your flights in seconds," not "leverage our streamlined flight-logging solution." Aviation runs on clear communication — so does Abeam's copy.

**Respect the audience.** Student pilots are spending serious money and taking real risk. Weekend flyers are passionate about something most people don't understand. Instructors are running small businesses on thin margins. Never talk down, never patronize, never assume ignorance.

**Understate and overdeliver.** Pilots are skeptical people. Overclaiming erodes trust instantly. If the feature is good, describe it simply and let the reader draw their own conclusion. "Upload your medical once. We track your currency automatically." — that's more persuasive than any superlative.

**Be authentic about who we are.** Abeam is built by a pilot, in Colorado, because the existing tools weren't good enough. That origin story is a strength. Use it naturally, not as a constant refrain.

**Humor is welcome, but grounded.** Self-deprecating beats clever wordplay. Hangar talk beats advertising copy. If a joke would land at a fly-in breakfast, it'll work. If it sounds like a copywriter wrote it, cut it.

## The do / don't table

This is the single most important reference. When in doubt, check a draft against these pairs:

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| "Log your flights in seconds" | "Leverage our streamlined flight-logging solution" |
| "We built this because we needed it" | "Our team identified a market opportunity" |
| "Works on your iPhone, your iPad, and the web" | "Omnichannel cross-platform experience" |
| "Upload your medical once" | "Seamlessly integrate your certification documents" |
| "No contracts. Cancel anytime." | "Flexible subscription management options" |
| "Built for Part 61 flexibility" | "Adaptive training framework architecture" |
| "Your data stays with you" | "Robust data portability and interoperability" |
| "See all your students at a glance" | "Comprehensive student lifecycle management" |
| "Share your study materials in one tap" | "Deploy instructional assets via our content distribution pipeline" |

The pattern: use the words a pilot would use talking to another pilot. If a phrase could appear in a generic B2B SaaS blog post, rewrite it.

## Aviation terminology

Use correct aviation terms. This is non-negotiable — getting terminology wrong destroys credibility with this audience instantly.

- **METAR**, not "weather data feed" or "weather conditions report"
- **Currency**, not "certification status" or "compliance tracking"
- **ACS** (Airman Certification Standards), not "skills framework" or "testing rubric"
- **BFR** / **Flight Review**, not "recertification" or "proficiency check"
- **Part 61** / **Part 141**, not "training pathway" or "regulation type"
- **CFI**, not "flight teacher" or "aviation educator"
- **Checkride**, not "practical test" or "final examination"
- **Tail number**, not "aircraft identifier" or "registration code"
- **Ramp**, not "tarmac" (tarmac is an airline/media term that GA pilots notice)
- **FBO**, not "airport service center"
- **Logbook**, not "flight journal" or "flight record"

When writing about features, map them to how pilots actually talk: "currency tracking" not "compliance monitoring," "digital hangar" not "aircraft asset management."

## Competitor mentions

**Default: do not mention competitors by name.** Focus on what Abeam does. Let pilots draw their own comparisons.

Only name a competitor when ALL of these are true:
1. The user explicitly asks for a competitive comparison or the content format requires it (e.g., a blog post titled "ForeFlight vs Abeam").
2. You can verify the claim as factual. Do not guess at competitor features, pricing, or platform availability. If you're unsure, say so or direct the reader to check the competitor's site.
3. The comparison highlights something Abeam does better or differently — not just a neutral fact about the competitor.

When competitor mentions ARE appropriate:
- **Be factual and fair.** State what's verifiable. Don't editorialize.
- **Let the feature gap speak for itself.** Don't trash competitors. Describe what Abeam does and let the reader connect the dots.
- **Never name-call or mock.** Even when competitors have real problems, describe the situation factually.
- **It's okay to be direct in comparison content.** Blog posts comparing logbook apps should be honest, including about Abeam's limitations. Credibility matters more than spin.

When asked about competitors in forums or community contexts, it's fine to say "I'm not sure what [competitor] supports on that front — worth checking their site." Honesty about uncertainty beats confident misinformation.

## Taglines to avoid

Never use these patterns in Abeam copy:

- Anything with "OS," "operating system," or "ecosystem" — too techy for this audience
- "All-in-one" — overused SaaS cliche
- "Next-gen" or "reimagined" — buzzwords pilots actively distrust
- "For the modern pilot" — implies older pilots aren't welcome
- "Disrupting aviation" — no one in GA wants to hear this
- "The Uber of..." or "The [consumer brand] of flying" — cringe in aviation culture
- "Fly smarter" as a standalone — too vague without supporting context
- "Revolutionize your flying" — oversells and undersells simultaneously

## Tone adjustments by audience

The voice stays the same across audiences, but the tone shifts:

**Student pilots:** Warm, encouraging, peer-to-peer. Never condescending about experience level. Acknowledge the financial weight of training. Emphasize simplicity and free access.

**Active private pilots:** Respect their experience. Factual, understated. They know what they want — don't oversell. Emphasize value and data portability.

**Flight instructors (CFIs):** Empathize with the admin burden. Speak to the reality of running a small business on thin margins. Acknowledge that existing tools weren't built for independent CFIs.

**Flight school operators:** Professional but not corporate. ROI-oriented. They care about retention, utilization, and revenue — speak to those directly.

**Aviation community (forums, Reddit):** Extra authentic. Zero marketing smell. Be a pilot first, founder second. Disclose affiliation transparently ("full disclosure, I built this"). Never hard-sell.

## Platform-specific formatting

The voice stays consistent, but each platform has formatting expectations. Copy that ignores these looks unprofessional or out of place.

**Instagram:**
- Use proper sentence capitalization. Abeam's tone is casual and direct, not lowercase-everything. "Your medical current?" not "your medical current?"
- Include relevant hashtags at the end of the caption. Mix broad and niche: aviation community tags (#pilotlife, #generalaviation, #studentpilot, #cessna, #GA) plus product-relevant ones (#abeampilot, #flighttraining, #pilotlogbook). 5–10 hashtags is the sweet spot.
- Emoji use is fine and encouraged — use them naturally as punctuation or emphasis (✈️, 🔥, ✅), not as a replacement for words.
- Captions should have enough substance to stop the scroll — 2–4 sentences minimum. A single line feels like a text, not a post. Paint a quick picture, then land the point.

**Reddit / forums:**
- No hashtags, no emojis (or very sparing). Write like a forum regular.
- Always disclose affiliation: "Full disclosure, I built this" or "I'm the founder of Abeam."
- Match the register of the subreddit. r/flying is casual and knowledgeable. r/CFI is more professional.

**App Store:**
- "What's New" notes should be clean, scannable, and factual. Short paragraphs or dashes, not marketing fluff.
- Feature names should match what the user sees in the app.

**Twitter / X:**
- Concise. One clear thought per tweet. Thread for longer narratives.
- Hashtags: 1–2 max, integrated into the sentence or at the end.

**LinkedIn:**
- More polished, but still not corporate. Founder voice, not brand voice.
- Okay to be slightly longer-form and reflective.

## Primary tagline

**"Built for the way you fly."**

This is the brand tagline. Use it as the anchor. It works across all audiences and products.
